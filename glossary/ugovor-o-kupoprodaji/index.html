What is Ugovor o kupoprodaji (Purchase Agreement)?

The Montenegrin property purchase and sale agreement — the notarised contract transferring ownership of real property from seller to buyer.

Key Facts — Ugovor o kupoprodaji (Purchase Agreement)
  • Must be in written form and notarised by a licensed Montenegrin notary
  • Legal title transfers only on registration in the Katastar nepokretnosti
  • Notary verifies title, identity, and encumbrances before certification
  • Distinguish the final deed from the preliminary predugovor
  • 3% real estate transfer tax generally applies on purchase

The ugovor o kupoprodaji (purchase and sale agreement) is the central legal document in a Montenegrin property transaction. It sets out the agreed sale price, payment terms, description of the property, condition of title, and the obligations of both buyer and seller.

Under Montenegrin law, contracts for the sale of real property must be in written form and solemnised by a licensed notary (notar). The notary verifies the identities of the parties, confirms the seller's registered ownership, ensures there are no undisclosed encumbrances, and certifies the document. An uncertified written contract is insufficient to transfer title.

After certification, the notary submits the application to register the transfer in the Real Estate Cadastre. Legal ownership passes only when the buyer's name is entered as registered owner — the certified contract itself is not sufficient to transfer title against third parties.

Montenegro distinguishes between a preliminary agreement (predugovor) and the final purchase agreement. A predugovor is often signed at the reservation stage with a deposit; it creates a contractual obligation to complete but does not transfer title. The final notarised ugovor o kupoprodaji is what triggers the registration process.

Transaction costs in Montenegro typically include the 3% real estate transfer tax (unless a VAT exemption applies), notary fees, and cadastre registration fees. Your Montenegrin lawyer should prepare a full cost estimate before you commit to the transaction.

Common Mistake: Treating a signed predugovor (preliminary agreement) as equivalent to full ownership. The preliminary agreement creates an obligation to complete, but you do not own the property until the final notarised deed is signed and registered in the cadastre.
Expert Tip: Insist on having your own Montenegrin property lawyer review the ugovor o kupoprodaji before the notarial appointment. The notary is neutral and will not negotiate contract terms on your behalf — your lawyer protects your specific interests.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sign a Montenegrin property deed by power of attorney?

Yes. A notarised power of attorney (punomoćje) granted to a Montenegrin lawyer or trusted representative allows them to sign the deed on your behalf without you attending the notary's office in person.

How long does the property registration process take after signing?

Registration in the Montenegrin cadastre typically takes between five and fifteen working days, depending on the regional office. Expedited processing is available in some offices for an additional fee.

Are there any restrictions on what foreigners can buy in Montenegro?

Foreign nationals can freely purchase most urban residential and commercial property. Restrictions may apply to agricultural land, protected zones, and areas near military installations. Always confirm with your Montenegrin lawyer before making an offer.
