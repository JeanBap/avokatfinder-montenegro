Ugovor o kupoprodaji (Purchase Agreement)

The notarially authenticated property purchase contract required for real estate transactions in Montenegro, which must be submitted to the cadastre for ownership registration.

An ugovor o kupoprodaji (purchase and sale agreement) is the legally binding contract for property transactions in Montenegro. Montenegrin law requires that property purchase agreements be prepared and authenticated by a Montenegrin notary (notar) to be valid for registration in the Real Estate Cadastre (Katastrar nepokretnosti). Without notarial authentication, a property sale agreement cannot be submitted for registration, and ownership does not legally transfer. This notarial requirement exists to protect both parties and ensure legal certainty for one of the most significant financial transactions most people ever undertake.

A complete Montenegrin ugovor o kupoprodaji includes the full identification of buyer and seller with all required personal identification details, a precise description of the property using its cadastral parcel number, the agreed purchase price and payment terms, provisions for any applicable mortgage discharge, warranties that the property is free of encumbrances (or a list of accepted encumbrances), the handover date, and the parties obligations with regard to transfer tax and notarial fees. The notar will review and authenticate each page, and the document is bound and stamped into an official notarial act.

The standard Montenegrin property transaction process begins with agreement on price, followed by a preliminary contract (predugovor) secured by an earnest money deposit (kapara). The kapara is typically 10% of the purchase price. Under Montenegrin law, if the buyer withdraws, the kapara is forfeited; if the seller withdraws, they must return double the kapara. This bilateral penalty makes the preliminary contract a meaningful commitment. The predugovor itself may or may not require notarial authentication, though having it notarised provides stronger legal protection for both parties.

Property transfer tax (porez na promet nepokretnosti) in Montenegro is charged at 3% of the agreed purchase price or the officially assessed property value, whichever is higher. This tax is normally paid by the buyer, though the parties can contractually agree otherwise. The tax must be paid within 15 days of the registration application being submitted to the cadastre. New-build properties purchased directly from a developer are often subject to VAT instead of transfer tax. Your notar or advokat will clarify which tax applies to your specific transaction.

Montenegro's property market has been particularly active with foreign buyers, especially from the EU, Russia, UK, and Middle East. The country has a relatively liberal regime for foreign property ownership: most nationalities can purchase residential and commercial real estate without restrictions. Agricultural land purchases face some restrictions. Montenegro's candidacy for EU membership and its existing use of the euro as its official currency have made it attractive for European investors. Always engage an independent Montenegrin advokat (lawyer) to conduct due diligence on the title, planning status, and development potential before committing to any purchase.

Key Facts

  • Must be notarially authenticated for cadastre registration
  • Preliminary contract (predugovor) typically secured by 10% kapara deposit
  • Property transfer tax is 3% of purchase price or assessed value
  • Foreign buyers can generally purchase residential property without restriction
  • Standard registration period is 15-30 days after application

Common Mistake

Many buyers sign the predugovor (preliminary contract) without conducting proper title due diligence on the katastrar, assuming problems can be sorted later. Issues discovered after paying the kapara — such as an undischarged hipoteka (mortgage), planning irregularities, or unresolved co-ownership disputes — are then very expensive to resolve. Always conduct full legal due diligence before signing any preliminary agreement and paying any deposit.

Practical Tip

Obtain a current urban planning certificate (urbanistički iskaz) for the property before purchase. This document confirms whether the property complies with current planning rules and whether any planned development nearby could affect your use. For coastal properties especially, check flood zone designations and building exclusion zones. Montenegro's coastal planning rules have been subject to significant changes and some properties marketed as buildable may not in fact have planning permission.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can foreigners freely buy property in Montenegro?

Yes, most foreign nationals can purchase residential and commercial property in Montenegro without special permits. Citizens of most countries can buy freely. There are some restrictions on agricultural land purchases. Montenegro uses the euro as its currency, making transactions straightforward for European buyers.

What is the property transfer tax rate in Montenegro?

Property transfer tax (porez na promet nepokretnosti) is 3% of the agreed purchase price or the officially assessed value, whichever is higher. For new-build properties purchased from developers, VAT typically applies instead. The tax is normally paid by the buyer within 15 days of the registration application submission.

How long does property registration take in Montenegro?

After the notarially authenticated purchase contract is submitted to the Real Estate Cadastre (Katastrar nepokretnosti), registration typically takes 15 to 30 working days. Expedited registration is available in some circumstances for a higher fee.
