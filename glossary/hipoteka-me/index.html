Hipoteka

Hipoteka is the Montenegrin term for a mortgage — a security right over real property registered in the Katastarski Registar giving the lender enforceable priority against the property if the borrower defaults.

In Montenegro, a hipoteka is created by a notarially verified agreement and registered with the Uprava za nekretnine. Once registered, it constitutes a real right (stvarno pravo) over the property, effective against any subsequent owner. The Central Bank of Montenegro (CBCG) regulates mortgage lending.

Montenegro uses the euro as its currency despite not being an EU member, which simplifies mortgage calculations for foreign buyers. Montenegrin banks offer euro-denominated hipoteka loans, typically requiring 20–30% down payment for residential purchases.

When buying Montenegrin property subject to an existing hipoteka, the purchase agreement should provide for simultaneous brisanje (cancellation) of the mortgage at closing. The buyer's notary coordinates the repayment and removal from the Katastarski Registar.

After repaying a Montenegrin hipoteka, the lender issues a saglasnost za brisanje (consent to cancellation). Filing this with the Uprava za nekretnine removes the hipoteka from the Katastarski Registar. Prompt filing prevents complications in future transactions.

Common Mistake: Completing a Montenegrin property purchase without confirming that the seller's hipoteka is cancelled at the Uprava za nekretnine. An uncancelled mortgage follows the property to the new owner.
Expert Tip: Structure the closing so the seller's hipoteka is cancelled simultaneously with the buyer's registration. Request a confirmed brisanje from the Uprava za nekretnine before releasing the full purchase price to the seller.

Montenegro does not impose a mortgage registration tax, making hipoteka establishment relatively cost-efficient. Legal and notarial fees for setting up a hipoteka are regulated and typically amount to 0.5–1% of the loan amount.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a saglasnost za brisanje in Montenegro?

A saglasnost za brisanje is the consent to cancellation issued by a Montenegrin mortgage lender after full repayment of the hipoteka debt. It is submitted to the Uprava za nekretnine to remove the mortgage entry from the Katastarski Registar.

What LTV ratio applies to Montenegrin mortgages?

Montenegrin banks typically lend up to 70–80% of the appraised value for residential hipoteka loans, requiring a minimum 20–30% deposit. The CBCG sets prudential limits and may adjust these based on macroprudential considerations.

Does Montenegro charge a mortgage registration tax?

Montenegro does not impose a specific hipoteka registration tax. The main costs associated with establishing a mortgage are notarial fees (regulated tariff) and Uprava za nekretnine registration fees, together typically amounting to under 1% of the loan amount.
