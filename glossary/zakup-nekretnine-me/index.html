Zakup Nekretnine

Zakup nekretnine is a Montenegrin property lease — the contractual arrangement by which a landlord grants a tenant the right to use real property for a defined period in exchange for regular rent payments.

Montenegrin tenancy law is primarily governed by the Zakon o obligacionim odnosima (Law on Obligations). Unlike countries with specific residential tenancy legislation, Montenegro relies more heavily on general contract law for lease arrangements, giving parties greater contractual freedom but fewer statutory tenant protections.

A zakup nekretnine agreement should be in writing and specify the property description, monthly zakupnina (rent), term, deposit amount, maintenance obligations, and notice provisions. For leases exceeding one year, notarial certification is advisable and required for registration in the Katastarski Registar.

Tenants in Montenegro have fewer statutory protections than in Western European countries. The zakup agreement is primarily governed by its terms, and eviction procedures — while requiring a court process — are generally faster than in more tenant-protective jurisdictions.

A zakup nekretnine registered in the Katastarski Registar is effective against third parties — particularly important if the property is sold during the lease. An unregistered lease may not bind a new owner who acquires without notice of the tenancy.

Common Mistake: Relying on an unregistered verbal zakup agreement for Montenegrin property letting. Without a written registered agreement, disputes over rent, deposits, or notice periods are difficult to resolve through Montenegrin courts.
Expert Tip: Register any Montenegrin lease exceeding one year with the Uprava za nekretnine. Registration protects the tenant against eviction on property sale and protects the landlord by creating a formal record of the agreed terms.

For foreign investors purchasing Montenegrin property as buy-to-let, the zakup nekretnine framework provides flexibility. Standard commercial lease terms allow free negotiation of rent, term, and indexation. Legal advice is recommended for leases exceeding one year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are there rent controls in Montenegro?

Montenegro does not impose statutory rent controls for private residential property. Zakupnina (rent) is freely negotiated between the parties. The market, particularly in coastal tourist areas, is driven by seasonal demand.

Does a Montenegrin property lease survive a sale?

A registered zakup nekretnine (registered in the Katastarski Registar) binds any subsequent owner of the property. An unregistered lease may not be enforceable against a new owner who acquires without notice of the tenancy.

What deposit can a Montenegrin landlord charge?

Montenegrin law does not prescribe a maximum deposit for residential lettings. Common practice in Montenegro is one to two months' rent as deposit, but the parties are free to agree any amount in the zakup nekretnine agreement.
